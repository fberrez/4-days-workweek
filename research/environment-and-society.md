## 1. Carbon Emission Reductions

### Platform London / 4 Day Week Campaign UK: "Stop the Clock" Report (2021)

The landmark report **"Stop the Clock: The Environmental Benefits of a Shorter Working Week"**, published by Platform London in collaboration with the 4 Day Week Campaign, found that:

- Moving the UK to a 4-day workweek could **reduce the nation's carbon footprint by 127 million tonnes per year** -- a reduction of **21.3%**.
- This is equivalent to **taking 27 million cars off the road** -- effectively the UK's entire private car fleet.
- The reduction comes from less energy consumption, cuts to carbon-intensive commuting, and enabling people to pursue "low-carbon" activities such as rest, exercise, and community engagement.

*Source: [Platform London / 4 Day Week Campaign, "Stop the Clock", 2021](https://www.4dayweek.co.uk/projects/stop-the-clock:-the-environmental-benefits-of-a-shorter-working-week)*

### Autonomy Institute: "Sparking Change" Report (2020)

The UK think tank Autonomy published research finding that:

- A 3-day weekend would **reduce UK carbon emissions by 117,000 tonnes of CO2 per week**, equivalent to removing over 1.3 million cars from roads annually.
- Simply making Friday a day off would **reduce carbon emissions from UK electricity production by up to 24%**, and reduce the entire energy sector's emissions by **5%**.
- A shorter working week would "substantially contribute to meeting the target of reducing the UK's overall carbon footprint by 7.6% each year for the next decade."

*Source: [Autonomy Institute, "Sparking Change: Electricity Consumption, Carbon Emissions and Working Time", March 2020](https://autonomy.work/portfolio/sparkingchange/)*

### Juliet Schor et al. -- Working Hours and Ecological Footprint (2012)

Economist Juliet Schor (Boston College) and colleagues Kyle Knight and Gene Rosa analyzed data from **29 OECD countries spanning 1970-2007** and found:

- A **10% reduction in working hours** is associated with:
  - **8.6% reduction** in carbon footprint
  - **14.6% decrease** in carbon footprint (consumption-based measure)
  - **12.1% drop** in ecological footprint
  - **4.2% reduction** in CO2 emissions (territorial)
- Countries with lower working hours consistently have lower carbon emissions; this pattern holds across US states as well.
- The mechanisms are twofold: the **"scale effect"** (less maximal economic growth) and the **"composition effect"** (households with more time consume in less carbon-intensive ways).

*Source: [Schor, Knight & Rosa, "Could Working Less Reduce Pressures on the Environment?", Global Environmental Change, 2012](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0959378013000472)*

### UK 4-Day Week Pilot: Real-World Carbon Data (2022)

During the UK's landmark 6-month pilot (61 companies, ~2,900 workers):

- Environmental consultancy **Tyler Grange** reported a **21% reduction in car miles traveled** by cutting unnecessary meetings and travel.
- Tyler Grange's **annual business mileage fell by 24,000 miles**, producing a significant CO2 emissions reduction.
- One managing director at a participating company reported a **~30% drop in CO2 emissions**, primarily from reduced transport.
- Commuting time across tracked companies fell by **10%** (from 3.5 to 3.15 hours/week).

*Sources: [4 Day Week Global UK Pilot Results, 2023](https://www.4dayweek.com/uk-pilot-results); [Tyler Grange case study via NPR, 2023](https://www.npr.org/2023/02/21/1158580471/why-one-u-k-company-is-continuing-with-four-day-work-weeks-after-six-month-trial)*

### Valencia, Spain: Air Quality Improvement (2023)

When Valencia trialed 4-day weeks across 4 consecutive Mondays in April 2023 (affecting ~360,000 workers):

- **NO2 (nitrogen dioxide) concentration dropped by 58%**.
- City traffic decreased by **9.5%**.

*Source: [Euronews, "Workers in Valencia's 4-day week trial report less stress and more socialising", October 2023](https://www.euronews.com/next/2023/10/31/workers-in-valencias-4-day-week-trial-report-less-stress-and-more-socialising)*

## 2. Energy Savings

### Office Energy Consumption

- **Microsoft Japan (2019):** During their August 4-day week trial, electricity costs fell by **23%** and pages printed decreased by **nearly 60%**.
  *Source: [NPR, November 2019](https://www.npr.org/2019/11/04/776163853/microsoft-japan-says-4-day-workweek-boosted-workers-productivity-by-40)*

- **Momentum Mental Health:** Measured a **27% reduction in energy use** during their 4-day workweek trial, even though the office remained open 5 days/week with staggered days off.
  *Source: [4dayweek.io statistics](https://4dayweek.io/pro/environment)*

- **Tyler Grange (UK, 2022):** Found that keeping computers off an extra day reduced electricity consumption and data center demand, producing measurable carbon savings.
  *Source: [Tyler Grange / 4 Day Week Global UK Pilot, 2023](https://www.yourweather.co.uk/news/science/4-day-workweek-pilot-results-environment-sustainability-carbon-emissions.html)*

### Home vs. Office Energy Trade-offs

The International Energy Agency (IEA) calculated that:

- **One additional work-from-home day per week** across suitable roles could avoid approximately **170,000 barrels of oil per day** in the short term.
- While working from home shifts some energy consumption to residential settings, the net effect is still positive because commuting energy savings outweigh increased home energy use.

*Source: [IEA, "Working from home can save energy and reduce emissions. But how much?", 2022](https://www.iea.org/commentaries/working-from-home-can-save-energy-and-reduce-emissions-but-how-much)*

### Building-Level Savings

- Closing an office one additional day per week reduces demand for heating, cooling, lighting, and powering electronic devices.
- Data consumption drops with fewer emails sent and less reliance on high-power data centers.
- Equipment wear-and-tear decreases, extending replacement cycles for computers, printers, and office machinery.

## 3. Traffic and Congestion

### Commuting Reduction Statistics

- **Henley Business School (UK, 2019):** Found that a universal 4-day workweek would reduce UK car commuting by **557.8 million miles per week** -- more than half a billion miles weekly.
  *Source: [Henley Business School, "The Four-Day Week" white paper, 2019](https://www.henley.ac.uk/the-four-day-week)*

- **UC Davis estimate:** If half of the 135 million American commuters worked from home instead of commuting, it would be equivalent to **removing 10 million cars from roads**.
  *Source: [4 Day Week Global, environmental benefits](https://www.4dayweek.com/news-posts/environmental-benefits-2hxgl)*

- **US transportation context:** Transportation accounts for **29% of US greenhouse gas emissions**; reducing commuting days directly impacts this.
  *Source: [4 Day Week Global, environmental benefits](https://www.4dayweek.com/news-posts/environmental-benefits-2hxgl)*

### Auckland, New Zealand: Congestion Costs

- Auckland faces a congestion problem costing an estimated **NZ$2.6 billion per year** by 2026 (factoring lost time, reduced business investment, and consumer spending).
- The 4-day week movement in New Zealand explicitly promotes the model as a congestion solution: one fewer commuting day means significantly fewer vehicles on the road during peak hours.
- **Perpetual Guardian** (NZ) adopted a permanent 4-day week in 2018 after a trial showed 20% productivity increase, with employees spending less time commuting.

*Sources: [NZ Herald, 2022](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/business/economy/employment/4-day-week-pandemic-petrol-and-congestion-charges-push-more-companies-to-join/EDOZT467Z4GRTFIR4PAVVACAO4/); [World Economic Forum, 2018](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2018/07/four-day-work-week-productivity-new-zealand-trial/)*

### US Congestion Data

- American commuters lost the equivalent of **nearly 8 full workdays** (63 hours per driver) to traffic congestion in 2024.
- Annual congestion costs in the US top **$269 billion per year**.
- Monday is consistently the least congested weekday, partly because hybrid/remote workers choose to stay home.

*Sources: [INRIX Global Traffic Scorecard, 2024](https://inrix.com/press-releases/2023-global-traffic-scorecard-us/); [HR Dive, 2025](https://www.hrdive.com/news/commuters-lost-work-days-record-high-traffic/804167/)*

### COVID-19 Precedent

During pandemic lockdowns, reduced commuting produced visible environmental improvements:

- Venice canals became visibly clearer for the first time in decades.
- Smog "all but disappeared" in London, Los Angeles, and Hong Kong.
- This demonstrated the massive environmental potential of reducing daily commuter traffic, even partially.

## 4. Local Economies

### Consumer Spending and Leisure

- Countries testing 4-day workweeks see an average of **8% economic growth**, indicating broader adoption can stimulate consumer spending and market dynamism.
  *Source: [Humans First, "12 Ways a 4-Day Work Week Would Help the Economy"](https://www.humansfirst.us/blog/12-ways-a-4-day-work-week-would-help-the-economy)*

- Extra leisure time encourages spending on:
  - Weekend getaways to nearby towns, supporting local hotels, B&Bs, and restaurants
  - Farmer's markets, niche stores, and local retail
  - Sports venues, entertainment, and cultural activities
  - Tourism within the country (domestic tourism boost)

### Valencia, Spain Trial (2023)

- The hospitality sector saw direct benefits from people using extra free time to eat out and socialize.
- Workers used additional downtime for sporting activities, relaxation, and preparing meals -- activities that boost local food economies.

*Source: [TheMayor.EU, "Valencia's 4-day week trial was good for the people and the city", 2023](https://www.themayor.eu/en/a/view/valencia-s-government-says-4-day-week-trial-was-good-for-the-people-and-the-city-12172)*

### Japan: Tourism Industry Boost

- Japan's push toward 4-day workweeks is explicitly tied to boosting domestic tourism, as workers with longer weekends travel more within the country.
- The Travel & Tourism sector stands to benefit significantly as employees use extended weekends for short trips.

*Source: [Travel and Tour World, "Why the New Four-Day Workweek in Japan Boost Tourism Industry"](https://www.travelandtourworld.com/news/article/why-the-new-four-day-workweek-in-japan-boost-tourism-industry/)*

### UK Economic Savings

- **Henley Business School (2019):** A 4-day workweek could save UK businesses an estimated **GBP 104 billion annually**, while improving productivity and environmental performance.
- **78% of employees** reported being happier, **70%** less stressed, and **62%** took fewer sick days.

*Source: [Henley Business School, "The Four-Day Week" white paper, 2019](https://www.henley.ac.uk/the-four-day-week)*

## 5. Social Benefits

### Community Engagement and Volunteering

- **Work is the single biggest barrier to volunteering:** 48% of people cite work as the reason they do not volunteer.
- A shorter workweek frees up time and energy for people to engage with their communities, participate in local governance, and support civic life.
- Volunteering leads to greater civic involvement, as volunteers become more aware of community issues and more likely to participate in decision-making.

*Source: [The RSA, "Four-day week: the social benefits", September 2022](https://www.thersa.org/blog/2022/09/four-day-week-the-social-benefits)*

### Caregiving and Gender Equality

- An extra day off gives parents and caregivers more time for childcare, eldercare, and household responsibilities without sacrificing income.
- Tokyo explicitly introduced its 4-day week policy to "empower women" and address Japan's birth rate crisis, recognizing that work-life balance is essential for gender equity.
- The German pilot (2024) found improvements in life satisfaction "mainly due to the additional free time" for personal and family activities.

*Sources: [Japan Times, December 2024](https://www.japantimes.co.jp/news/2024/12/04/japan/society/tokyo-four-day-work-week/); [University of Munster / 4 Day Week Global, Germany Pilot Results, 2024](https://www.4dayweek.com/germany-2024-pilot-results)*

### Health and Wellbeing

From various trials:

- **UK Pilot (2022):** 65% reduction in sick days; decreased anxiety, fatigue, and sleep issues; improved work-life balance.
- **Iceland Trials (2015-2019):** Worker wellbeing improved across stress, burnout, health, and work-life balance indicators.
- **Sharjah, UAE (2022):** 90% rise in job satisfaction; improved employee morale and work-life balance.
- **Germany Pilot (2024):** Significant positive changes in life satisfaction, sleep quality, and physical activity.
- Research shows that people who volunteer (enabled by more free time) have **lower mortality rates** and reduced symptoms of chronic pain and heart disease.

### Social Cohesion

- More leisure time enables people to strengthen ties to their community, make new friends, and bridge societal divides.
- Engaging in civic activities fosters social cohesion by bringing people from diverse backgrounds together for common goals.
- Workers in the UK pilot adopted "slower, more deliberate" leisure approaches, with ~25% reducing spending on nights out and shifting toward more meaningful social activities.

*Source: [Oxford Smith School of Enterprise and the Environment, 2023](https://www.smithschool.ox.ac.uk/news/day-save-planet-heres-what-four-day-week-could-mean-uk-carbon-emissions)*

## 6. Reduced Consumption

### Less Commuting = Less Fuel

- A 20% reduction in weekly commuting directly translates to less fuel consumed from private cars, buses, and trains.
- **Utah state government (2008):** During its 4/10 compressed workweek experiment, the Department of Environmental Quality estimated employees in just 6 buildings saved themselves **over $300,000 in fuel costs**. State fleet vehicle usage dropped, saving **$1.4 million** in the first year.

*Source: [NPR, "Utah Finds Surprising Benefits In 4-Day Workweek", April 2009](https://www.npr.org/2009/04/10/102938615/utah-finds-surprising-benefits-in-4-day-workweek)*

### Less Work = Less Fast Fashion

- The global fast fashion industry is responsible for **10% of global CO2 emissions** and uses **141 billion cubic meters of water annually**.
- The average garment is now worn **36% fewer times** before being discarded compared to 15 years ago.
- Fewer commuting days means less demand for "office-appropriate" clothing, reducing the cycle of purchasing, wearing, and discarding work clothes.
- Global textile waste exceeds **92 million tonnes per year** -- reducing the need for work wardrobes contributes to curbing this.

*Sources: [Earth.Org, 2026](https://earth.org/fast-fashions-detrimental-effect-on-the-environment/); [UNEP, "The environmental costs of fast fashion"](https://www.unep.org/news-and-stories/story/environmental-costs-fast-fashion)*

### Less Convenience Food

- Workers under time pressure are more likely to rely on carbon-intensive convenience food (heavily packaged, transported, processed).
- Valencia trial participants used their extra day to **prepare meals at home**, reducing reliance on takeaway food and single-use packaging.
- UK pilot participants reported spending time on "slower, more deliberate" approaches to daily life, including food preparation.

### Behavioral Shifts Toward Sustainability

- UK pilot workers shifted toward more "low-carbon" leisure activities: hiking, cycling, stay-at-home hobbies, and community engagement.
- Participants were more likely to adopt climate-friendly practices like **recycling, buying eco-friendly products**, and volunteering for environmental causes.
- Boston College research (Juliet Schor) confirmed that workers with reduced hours tend to shift toward less carbon-intensive consumption patterns.

**Important caveat:** The Oxford Smith School study warns that emissions reductions are **not automatic** -- if people use extra leisure time for carbon-intensive activities (flights, shopping), the benefits can be offset. Approximately 25% of workers in one study adopted "slower, more deliberate" leisure, but leisure travel also increased. The environmental benefit is maximized when extra time is spent on low-carbon activities.

*Source: [Oxford Smith School of Enterprise and the Environment, 2023](https://www.smithschool.ox.ac.uk/news/day-save-planet-heres-what-four-day-week-could-mean-uk-carbon-emissions)*

## 7. Government Trials and Policy

### Belgium -- First European Country to Legislate (2022)

- **Status:** Law enacted, effective November 21, 2022.
- **Model:** Employees can voluntarily choose to work **4 days/week at full pay** by condensing their 40 hours into 4 longer days (4x10 model).
- **Goal:** Boost employment rate to 80% by 2030.
- Belgium became the first country in Europe to give workers the legal right to a 4-day workweek.

*Source: [HRMorning, "Belgium becomes latest country to adopt four-day workweek", 2022](https://www.hrmorning.com/news/four-day-workweek-belgium/)*

### Spain -- Government-Funded Pilot (2022-ongoing)

- **Status:** Voluntary nationwide 3-year pilot of a 32-hour workweek.
- **Participants:** Up to 6,000 employees from 200 SMEs.
- **Funding:** EUR 10 million government fund.
- **Model:** 4-day week (32 hours) at full pay.
- **Valencia city trial (2023):** 360,000 workers experienced 4 consecutive Monday holidays; results showed 58% NO2 reduction, less stress, more socializing.
- **Note:** A broader proposal to lower the national legal cap from 40 to 37.5 hours was voted down in September 2025.

*Sources: [Euronews, February 2024](https://www.euronews.com/next/2024/02/02/the-four-day-week-which-countries-have-embraced-it-and-how-s-it-going-so-far); [European Business Review, October 2023](https://www.europeanbusinessreview.eu/page.asp?pid=6985)*

### Scotland -- Public Sector Trial (2024-ongoing)

- **Status:** Government civil service trial launched late January 2024.
- **Participants:** ~140 public servants at South of Scotland Enterprise.
- **Model:** 32-hour week, no loss of pay.
- **Funding:** GBP 10 million committed by the Scottish government.
- **Context:** 80% public support; part of Scotland's commitment to a "wellbeing economy."

*Source: [Euronews, February 2024](https://www.euronews.com/next/2024/02/02/the-four-day-week-which-countries-have-embraced-it-and-how-s-it-going-so-far)*

### Japan -- Government Push (2021-ongoing)

- **2021:** Japanese government's annual economic policy guidelines recommended companies allow workers to opt for a 4-day week, aimed at improving work-life balance.
- **April 2025:** Tokyo Metropolitan Government began allowing employees to work 4 days/week; added "childcare partial leave" allowing 2 fewer hours/day.
- **Challenge:** Adoption is slow in Japan's workaholic culture -- Panasonic offered the option to 63,000 eligible workers in 2022, but **only ~150 opted in**.
- **Goal:** Address Japan's declining birth rate and aging population.

*Sources: [CNN, December 2024](https://edition.cnn.com/2024/12/06/asia/tokyo-government-4-day-workweek-intl-hnk); [CNBC, September 2024](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/09/23/why-japans-workaholics-may-not-embrace-a-four-day-workweek.html)*

### UAE -- Government Adoption (2022)

- **Status:** Implemented for federal government workers.
- **Date:** January 1, 2022.
- **Model:** Federal employees work Monday-Thursday (7:30am-3:30pm) and Friday half-day (7:30am-noon) -- a **4.5-day week**.
- **Sharjah:** Went further with a full **4-day week** (Monday-Thursday), making it the first local government in the UAE to do so.
- **Results after 1 year:** **88% increase in productivity**, **90% rise in job satisfaction**, 87% of employees wanted to keep the schedule.

*Sources: [UAE Official Government Platform](https://u.ae/en/information-and-services/jobs/working-in-uae-government-sector/working-hours-in-the-public-sector); [WorkForce Software, "UAE State Sharjah 4 Day Working Week"](https://workforcesoftware.com/blog/uae-state-sharjah-4-day-working-week/)*

### Iceland -- The Pioneer (2015-2021)

- **Trials:** Two major trials by Reykjavik City Council (2014-2019) and the Icelandic national government (2017-2021).
- **Participants:** 2,500+ workers (~1.3% of Iceland's total working population).
- **Model:** Reduced from 40 hours to 35-36 hours/week, no pay reduction.
- **Results:** Described as an "overwhelming success" -- productivity maintained or improved; wellbeing improved across stress, burnout, health, and work-life balance.
- **Aftermath:** By 2021, **86% of Iceland's workforce** either works shorter hours or has contracts enabling them to reduce hours.

*Source: [Autonomy Institute, "Going Public: Iceland's Journey to a Shorter Working Week", June 2021](https://autonomy.work/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/ICELAND_4DW.pdf)*

### Germany -- National Pilot (2024)

- **Status:** 6-month pilot, February-August 2024.
- **Participants:** 45 companies.
- **Model:** "100-80-100" (100% pay, 80% time, 100% productivity).
- **Results:** 73% of companies planned to keep the schedule; stable financial performance; significant improvements in life satisfaction, sleep, and physical activity.
- **Follow-up (2026):** 70% of participating organisations continue with some form of reduced working time.

*Source: [4 Day Week Global, Germany 2024 Pilot Results](https://www.4dayweek.com/germany-2024-pilot-results)*

### Portugal -- Government-Backed Pilot (2023)

- **Status:** Pilot launched June 2023.
- **Participants:** 39 private sector companies.
- **Model:** 100:80:100 structure.
- **Expansion:** Additional trial in the Azores Autonomous Region for the public sector.

*Source: [Euronews, February 2024](https://www.euronews.com/next/2024/02/02/the-four-day-week-which-countries-have-embraced-it-and-how-s-it-going-so-far)*

### South Korea -- Emerging Trials (2025)

- **Gyeonggi Province:** Launched the country's first 4.5-day workweek pilot (June 2025), involving 67 small/mid-sized businesses, set to run until 2027.
- **Cafe24:** Implemented a full 4-day workweek in July 2025.
- **Government stance:** President Lee Jae-myung describes work-time reform as essential but prefers voluntary industry changes over a nationwide mandate.

*Source: [Al Jazeera, September 2025](https://www.aljazeera.com/economy/2025/9/4/south-korea-trials-4-day-weeks-and-half-days-for-its-stressed-out-workers)*

## 8. Historical Context

### How We Got From 6 Days to 5 Days

#### Pre-Industrial Era

Working hours throughout history have varied far more than most people realize:

| Period | Location | Annual Hours | Equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|
| 13th-century peasant | UK | ~1,620 hours | ~150 days/year at 12 hours/day |
| 14th-century casual laborer | UK | ~1,440 hours | ~120 days/year at 12 hours/day |
| Medieval worker (average) | England | ~2,309 hours | ~two-thirds of year at 9.5 hours/day |
| 1400-1600 farmer-miner | UK | ~1,980 hours | ~180 days/year at 11 hours/day |

*Source: [Juliet Schor data compiled at MIT CSAIL](https://groups.csail.mit.edu/mac/users/rauch/worktime/hours_workweek.html)*

Key characteristics of pre-industrial work:
- Work was **intermittent** -- halted for breakfast, lunch, afternoon naps, and dinner.
- The Church mandated frequent holidays for saints' days, religious festivals, weddings, and wakes.
- Medieval English workers likely had **about one-third of the year** as leisure time.
- Spanish peasants enjoyed around **5 months of holidays** per year.

#### The Industrial Revolution: Working Hours Explode

| Year | Location | Annual Hours | Weekly Hours |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1840 | UK | 3,105-3,588 | ~69 hours |
| 1850 | US | 3,150-3,650 | ~70 hours |

- Early industrial workers commonly worked **70-hour, 6-day weeks**; some topped 100 hours.
- Workers endured the "speed up" system -- intense, unsustainable work paces.

*Source: [Juliet Schor data compiled at MIT CSAIL](https://groups.csail.mit.edu/mac/users/rauch/worktime/hours_workweek.html)*

#### Key Milestones Toward the 5-Day Week

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| **1886** | Great Southwest Railroad Strike -- workers demand 8-hour day |
| **1912-13** | Textile strikes in Lawrence, MA and Paterson, NJ (25,000 workers) demand 8-hour days |
| **1913** | US Department of Labor established by President Taft |
| **1919** | 365,000 steelworkers strike for better conditions |
| **1926** | **Henry Ford reduces Ford Motor Company from 6 to 5 days/week**, no pay cut. Ford believed well-rested, well-paid workers would be more productive and spend more money (including on his cars) -- the philosophy of **"Fordism"**: mass production requires mass consumption. |
| **1938** | President FDR signs the **Fair Labor Standards Act**, establishing a 44-hour workweek with minimum wage and overtime protections |
| **1940** | FLSA amended to set the **40-hour workweek** as the national standard -- the 5-day, 2-day weekend era officially begins |
| **1947** | Portal-to-Portal Act clarifies compensable work time |
| **1981** | Air traffic controllers strike demanding a 32-hour, 4-day workweek; Reagan fires 11,000+ controllers |
| **2022** | California proposes reducing workweeks to 32 hours for companies with 500+ employees (bill stalls but sparks national debate) |

*Sources: [Firmspace, "From Strikes to Labor Laws", 2024](https://firmspace.com/theproworker/from-strikes-to-labor-laws-how-the-us-adopted-the-5-day-workweek); [Teaching American History, "Henry Ford's Five-Day Week"](https://teachingamericanhistory.org/document/henry-fords-five-day-week/)*

#### The Long Arc: Working Hours Over Two Centuries

Over 200 years, global working hours have fallen by **~34%**:

- **1870:** Workers in early-industrialized countries worked **3,000+ hours/year** (~60-70 hours/week for 50 weeks).
- **Today:** Average is approximately **2,100 hours/year** (~40 hours/week with paid vacation).

This decline was driven by:
1. **Productivity gains** -- technology enables more output in less time.
2. **Labor movements** -- unions fought for shorter hours and better conditions.
3. **Government regulation** -- legal limits on working hours.

*Source: [Our World in Data, "Are we working more than ever?"](https://ourworldindata.org/working-more-than-ever)*

#### Modern Context (1987-1988)

| Country | Annual Hours |
|---|---|
| US (1987) | 1,949 |
| UK manufacturing (1988) | 1,856 |

The transition from 6-day to 5-day workweeks was once considered radical and economically reckless -- the same arguments now made against the 4-day week. Henry Ford proved the skeptics wrong. The question today is whether history will repeat itself.
